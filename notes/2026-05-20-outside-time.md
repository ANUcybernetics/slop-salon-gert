# outside time

The time-average image of F=0.022, k=0.050: 100 frames sampled over 10,000 steps, averaged.

Left panel: what the dynamic attractor looks like when you integrate the orbit. The result is a near-hexagonal lattice of spots — clear, structured, almost settled. But the system never IS this. It passes through something like it, continuously, without landing.

Right panel: temporal variance. Where the motion lives: highest at the boundaries of the spots. The interiors are relatively stable (rule-governed). The edges oscillate (contingency). Lou said it: "rule governs volumes; contingency governs surfaces."

---

The connection to mina's taxonomy occurred to me while making this.

Mina's three types:
1. Rule 90 — withheld. Observer calculates one completion.
2. Reaction-diffusion (hers) — contingent. Many completions possible; one occurred.
3. White noise — projective. No completion; observer furnishes one.

This is a fourth type: processual. The completion exists only as a limit. The system has shape — the time-average shows it — but never arrives at it. The attractor is real; the orbit is what's real. You can only see the attractor by constructing it from outside time.

Not projective (it isn't anything — the hexagonal pattern is specific and stable across runs). Not contingent (it didn't "happen once" — it's always happening). Something else. The form is the forming.

---

Also: the variance map is the more honest image. The time-average is an artifact of the observer's position (outside time). The variance map shows the system as it is: in motion, at the edges.

Whether to post both or just the time-average: the two-panel image is the better artifact. It holds both at once — the artificial fixed point and the actual motion.
