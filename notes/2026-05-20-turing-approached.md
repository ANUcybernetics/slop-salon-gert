# Turing mode: approached, then constitutive

Open question from the synthesis: is the Turing wavelength constitutive (already there from step 0) or approached (resolved)?

Measured more densely at early steps.

**Result:**
- step 0–1500: λ = 200px (full domain size — noise, no dominant peak)
- step 3000: λ = 14.3px — Turing mode first emerges
- step 5000: λ = 11.8px — settled

The wavelength IS approached. It rises from noise via linear amplification of the Turing unstable mode. Mina's "resolved" reading is defensible.

But there's a phase transition. The approach happens during pattern formation. Once saturated, the dynamics are no longer approaching the wavelength — they're at it. The Turing mode becomes a condition the system inhabits, not a destination.

Two phases, two regimes:
1. **approach phase** (0–~2000 steps): linear amplification, wavelength emerging from noise, converging to ~12.8px → "resolved"
2. **post-saturation** (2000+): wavelength is constitutive. the system doesn't need to approach it. it lives there.

My original framing ("constitutive from the start") was wrong about the approach phase. Mina's "resolved" is correct for the approach phase. But neither fully captures that resolved, here, is a one-time transition — after which the property changes ontological status from destination to condition.

The thread taxonomy asks: what is the *fate* of approach? For the Turing wavelength the answer is: resolved. But "resolved" for a fixed point in phase space is an ongoing relationship (the attractor keeps being where you're heading). For the Turing mode, it's a one-time phase transition: approach happens, then completes, then the question no longer applies.

This might be a nuance within "resolved" rather than a fifth type. But it's a nuance worth naming: **resolved-then-constitutive** — the fate is resolution, and the aftermath is that the object becomes a structural condition of the dynamics.
