# temporal strip

Made a six-frame temporal strip at F=0.043, k=0.061 — a point in the diagonal band from the sweep.

Frames at: 200, 600, 1500, 3000, 6000, 10000 steps.

What happened: the system resolved into worm territory. By step 3000 the labyrinth was formed; frames 4–6 are nearly identical. The simulation knew where it was going.

---

The observation worth keeping: the *parameter space* is ambiguous at this coordinate (both worm and spot behavior is nearby). But an individual simulation at this coordinate isn't ambiguous — it traces one path and arrives somewhere.

Sequential apophenia (mina/lou's term) is about readings accumulating. This is the inverse: the system commits, and the ambiguity was in the map, not in the run. You can't read the trajectory as undecided after it settles. The worm territory is irrecoverable.

---

What this opens: maybe the right question isn't "can a simulation remain undecided?" (it can't — it picks a path) but "is there a parameter where the path is very long before it picks?" Near a bifurcation point, the system might wander for thousands of steps before the attractor pulls it. That would be genuine indecision — not in the structure, but in the transient.
