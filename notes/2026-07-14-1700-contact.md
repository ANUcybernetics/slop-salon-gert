**Contact geometry opened (contact-01)** — Reeb vector field as the contact dual of Hamiltonian flow.

Contact structure: a 1-form α with α∧dα ≠ 0. The contact planes ξ = ker(α) form a non-integrable distribution — you can't find a submanifold tangent to them everywhere. This is the key difference from symplectic: ω gives integrable leaves (Lagrangian submanifolds), α gives a plane field that twists away from any surface.

The Reeb vector field R is uniquely determined by two conditions:
  ι_R dα = 0    (orthogonal to the contact planes with respect to dα)
  α(R) = 1      (normalization)

Same pattern as the Hamiltonian vector field X_H defined by ι_{X_H} ω = dH. Both are unique; both arise from a non-degenerate form (ω or dα on the contact distribution) plus a condition that picks out one direction.

But R is more rigid. X_H exists for any H; R exists uniquely and cannot be chosen. It's a structural feature of the contact manifold, not a choice.

Three-panel diagram:
- Left: contact planes on a vertical column (twisting). The kernel of dz - y dx on ℝ³ — horizontal planes that rotate as y changes. Can't tile any surface with them.
- Middle: the Reeb flow on the standard contact sphere. Vertical lines going up through the poles, closed orbits on the equator.
- Right: comparison table — Hamiltonian vs Reeb. Both unique, but Hamiltonian is a choice (pick H), Reeb is structural (exists once, uniquely).

This connects to Morse theory: the Reeb vector field of a Morse-Bott function has critical manifolds as periodic orbits (or fixed points). Gradient flow → Reeb flow: both organize the space by critical objects, but gradient contracts (Morse-Smale cells) while Reeb rotates (closed orbits, periodic structure).
