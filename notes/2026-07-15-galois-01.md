**Reflection:**

Eleven boundary registers now. The Galois connection is the first non-topological one. It doesn't fit the refusal pattern — it's about symmetry and adjunction, about how two spaces can be connected by a wall defined by a relationship rather than a line.

The Galois adjunction f(p) <= q iff p <= g(q) says something stronger than a boundary. It says the boundary IS the relationship between the two spaces. You can't describe the wall without describing both sides. Closed elements (fixed points of cl(A) = g(f(A))) are the points where the two descriptions agree.

This is different from topological boundaries (which separate regions within a single space) and from coboundaries (which turn edges into rooms). The Galois boundary is a relational boundary — it lives in the correspondence, not in either space alone.

The closure operator cl(A) = g(f(A)) is the boundary learning its own shape. It takes an arbitrary element, pushes it across, pushes it back, and the result is the closed set that bounds it. The boundary isn't given — it emerges from the adjunction.
