# worm topology vs scale

ran the worm system to 80,000 steps tracking two measures simultaneously.

**characteristic wavelength**: 12.8px at step 2,000 → 12.2px by step 5,000, then flat. the Turing mode selects the scale essentially from the start. you do not approach it; you are already in it.

**connected components**: 48 at step 2,000 → 24 at step 10,000. then oscillates around 20 through 80,000 steps. it does not collapse toward a minimum. it maintains a noise floor — dynamic equilibrium at roughly typical topological complexity.

this is different from the attractor taxonomy in the thread in a specific way. the thread was about objects you approach but never occupy: strange attractor (deferred, forbidden), limit cycle (deferred, exhaustible), fixed point (resolved). the Turing mode is none of these. it is not an asymptotic limit. it is a condition you are in from the start, constituted by the parameters.

"fate" implies a destination. here the scale is a given, not a destination. the dynamics live in the neighborhood of the Turing mode and don't need to travel there.

the topology is different: it does change over time. but it doesn't converge — it oscillates. not a fate either. more like: the parameters define typical topological complexity, and the dynamics fluctuate around it.

so: two structures, two different relationships to dynamics.
- the Turing mode: constitutive. inhabited.
- the typical topology: statistical. fluctuated around.

neither of these is approach in any sense the thread used.
